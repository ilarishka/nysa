Representation in New York State Assembly

The "Assembly Members" tab of the the New York State Assembly website has the feel of a 90s yearbook. Each representative gets a spot. Pictures, titles, email addresses; all that is missing is a cheesy inspirational quote. The assemblymen stares back at the reader from the screen, with smiling faces and tailored suits. What one might also notice — or maybe expect — is that the majority are white. How many, you ask? 111 out of 149.

Racial issues have taken center stage in New York in the past few months, especially following the killing of Eric Garner. A black man from Staten Island, Garner died after policeman Daniel Pantaleo put him in chokehold last July. The decision by the Staten Island grand jury not to indict Pantaleo gave way to a series of demonstrations against police brutality and racial profiling. Members of the black community across the country feel misunderstood and misrepresented, and the fact has crept its way back into public awareness most prominently this week in Baltimore.

Under these circumstances, it might be surprising to hear that the black community is the most fairly represented group in the New York State Assembly, out of the four biggest racial and ethnic groups in the state: white, black, latino and Asian. In fact, a close look at the distribution of the state population along ethnic and racial lines reveals how the only case in which there is a close correspondence between number of representatives and share of the electorate belonging to a certain group is with the black population. In particular, 16.11 percent of seats in the assembly for 17.50 percent of black population across the state.

Things are much worse for latinos and Asians, who are significantly underrepresented. Asians — who represent 8.20 percent of the whole population — get one single seat (0.67 percent of the total) in the current legislature, while only 13 representatives (8.72 percent) come from a latino background. White assemblymen take the lion share with 74.50 percent of seats even though only 57.20 percent of the population belongs to this racial category.

Challenges and limits

The challenges when talking about race are many. Throw politics in the mix and the task can be even more daunting. One of the obvious limits of this kind of research is the lack of clear notions of race. The United States Census Bureau, for example, classifies whites, blacks and Asians as belonging to a race, while Latinos are described as a group that can belong to any race. But when we look at the overall population in the state of New York, we notice that the majority of Latinos identify exclusively as such. Latinos might not technically be a race, but the exclusivist nature of their group makes them comparable for the purposes of this study.

In collecting data I relied on the pictures available on the assembly's website, the origin of their names and, whenever that was not enough, I browsed the internet in search of further proof of a reesentative's racial or ethnic profile. The method is still susceptible to error and does not keep into account the possibility that a person might identify with more than one race, but can at least provide a general idea of how serious underepresentation is for certain groups.
